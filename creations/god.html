Part III · Essay
An Allegorical Algebra of God

A matrix reading of twenty models of ultimacy.

The Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy's entry on "God and Other Ultimates" opens with a definition that applies directly to everything in these two tables: to be ultimate is to be the most fundamentally real, most valuable, or most deeply fulfilling among all that there is or could be — metaphysically, axiologically, and soteriologically. The entry's author, Jeanine Diller, following John Schellenberg, uses these three dimensions to organise a vast landscape of models: models of Brahman, models of God, models of the Dao and beyond. What the tables above do is something complementary. They encode twenty models of ultimacy — ten philosophical, ten prophetic-poetic — in a structured grid, and invite a form of reading that the SEP entry does not attempt: the reading of that grid as a matrix, with all the algebraic properties that mathematical objects of that kind possess. The two approaches are not competitors. The SEP entry asks what the models say. This essay asks what the structure of the models reveals when the models are arranged in a 10 × 10 array and the properties of that array are examined.

A matrix is a rectangular array of values arranged in rows and columns. Each cell encodes a relationship between a row element and a column element; the full array encodes all such relationships simultaneously and can be analysed as a single mathematical object. The Philosopher Matrix has ten philosophers as columns and ten properties of ultimacy as rows: Existence, Consciousness, Singularity, Relation, Transcendence, Causality, Eternity, Knowability, Morality, Humanity. The Prophet Matrix has the same shape but different inhabitants: ten prophets and sacred poets as columns, ten revelatory properties as rows: Nature, Revelation, Singularity, Creation, Transcendence, Demand, Justice, Love, Salvation, Destiny. The cells of both matrices are not numbers but ideas — short labels in the table, long arguments in the panels behind them. They are positions in a conceptual space. Treating these tables as matrices is not a metaphor grafted onto them from outside: it is a recognition of what they already are, given that each cell encodes exactly one relationship between exactly one thinker and exactly one property of ultimacy.

Once the tables are matrices, their standard algebraic properties become meaningful descriptions of the intellectual space they define. The trace — the sum of the diagonal entries — captures what each thinker says about the property that most characterises them when the rows and columns are ordered to match. The determinant measures whether any column can be expressed as a combination of the others — whether any thinker is philosophically redundant. The eigenvalues identify the conceptual directions the matrix preserves under transformation: the ideas that survive when all the intellectual pressure of the tradition is applied simultaneously. The corresponding eigenvectors name the thinkers who most purely embody those invariant directions. The transpose reverses the reading, treating properties as thinkers and thinkers as properties, revealing what each property says when it passes through every mind in the table. The inverse — the transformation that undoes the matrix — represents, in this allegorical algebra, the state before thought: the silence from which each tradition began. The singular value decomposition (SVD) factors the matrix into its fundamental axes of variance, ranking the directions along which thinkers most differ from one another. And principal component analysis (PCA) projects the high-dimensional space of the table onto two dimensions while preserving as much structure as possible, producing a map on which each thinker can be located and their distances from one another read off. None of this is numerically precise in the usual sense: the cells are not scalars drawn from a field. What is being practised here is allegorical algebra — using the rigorous structure of linear algebra as a lens through which to read intellectual history, treating convergence as alignment, divergence as orthogonality, and the dominant patterns of a tradition as its eigenvectors. The algebra produces not numbers but descriptions, and descriptions of a precision and geometry that ordinary prose cannot easily reach.

Begin with the trace of the Philosopher Matrix — the diagonal cells in which each thinker answers the property that most defines them. Laozi on Existence: the Way unnamed before all things, not a being but the unnameable ground that makes both being and non-being possible. Plato on Consciousness: the Demiurge who wills and calculates, the Form of the Good that simply radiates — two divine structures in permanent unresolved tension. Nagarjuna on Singularity: unity itself is empty, no fixed essence anywhere, not even the concept of oneness has an inherent referent. Plotinus on Relation: the world overflows from the One by necessity rather than decision, as light from a flame that did not choose to shine. Shankara on Transcendence: Brahman transcends by being all — both and neither, the paradox dissolving once you see why. Maimonides on Causality: God is not merely the first cause of motion but the cause of being itself, the ground of creatio continua. Ibn Arabi on Eternity: all things exist first as eternal archetypes in God's self-knowledge before they are disclosed in external existence. Spinoza on Knowability: God is fully knowable through reason alone, the geometric method is the only theology required. Kant on Morality: goodness is prior to God, the moral law is not derived from divine commands but is binding on God and humans alike. Whitehead on Humanity: genuine reciprocity — the soul gives to God as God gives, nothing genuinely lived is ever lost. The trace of the Philosopher Matrix is not a number but a declaration stretched across twenty-five centuries. Summed, it reads as a single sentence: the divine is the unnamed ground, it radiates without deciding, unity itself is empty of inherent essence, the world overflows by necessity, transcendence and immanence collapse into the same fact, God sustains being itself at every moment, all possibilities exist eternally in divine self-knowledge, the divine is fully rational, moral goodness precedes its guarantor, and God grows richer with every soul that is received and preserved. That sentence is the philosophical tradition's longest continuous thought about ultimacy.

The determinant of the Philosopher Matrix is metaphorically non-zero: no column is linearly dependent on the others, no philosopher is redundant. This is the most basic structural claim the matrix makes about the tradition — it is full-rank, genuinely ten-dimensional, spanning the full conceptual space of what rational inquiry about ultimacy can reach. The SEP entry's taxonomy of models illuminates exactly why this is so. Laozi cannot be derived from Spinoza even though both arrive at a form of radical immanence, because Laozi's immanence is the immanence of emptiness and wu wei — spontaneous self-arising with no transitive cause — while Spinoza's is the immanence of logical necessity, every mode following from divine nature as a theorem follows from axioms. Nagarjuna cannot be derived from Kant even though both dissolve positive predicates of God, because Nagarjuna dissolves the questioner alongside the question — his Madhyamaka acid corrodes even the negations of negative theology — while Kant preserves the rational subject and merely relocates God from theoretical to practical reason. Whitehead cannot be derived from Ibn Arabi even though both insist on genuine divine responsiveness to the world, because Whitehead's consequent nature is a temporally growing reception of actual occasions while Ibn Arabi's divine self-disclosure operates through eternal archetypes that have always existed in God's self-knowledge and are merely disclosed, not newly acquired. The ten columns are independent. The matrix spans everything.

The eigenvalues of the Philosopher Matrix identify the directions it preserves under transformation — the ideas that survive when all ten thinkers are pressed simultaneously on all ten properties and the matrix is, metaphorically, multiplied by itself. The dominant eigenvalue belongs to Singularity. When you push the whole tradition at once and look for what remains invariant, the insistence on divine unity is what survives. Laozi, Plotinus, Shankara, Maimonides, Ibn Arabi, and Spinoza converge on this with a unanimity that is almost alarming, and from entirely different directions: Plotinus by showing that internal complexity would make the One depend on its components; Maimonides by showing that multiple attributes would make God composite and thus contingent; Shankara by showing that Advaita — not-two — is the only metaphysical description adequate to pure Being-Consciousness-Bliss; Spinoza by showing that there can be only one substance of which everything else is a mode. Even Nagarjuna's dissolution preserves this structure negatively: there is one emptiness, even if emptiness is itself empty. The second eigenvalue belongs to Eternity: across nine of ten columns, God is outside time. The Philosopher Matrix is, in Diller's terms, dominated by models that are at minimum metaphysically ultimate — the most fundamental fact about the nature of things — and that fact is characterised by unity and atemporality above all else.

The eigenvectors corresponding to these dominant eigenvalues name the thinkers who most purely embody them. Along the Singularity axis, the eigenvector runs through Plotinus: his account of the One as the source of all unity without itself being a unit, as the archetype of simplicity that makes every other simplicity possible, is the most thoroughgoing account of divine unity in the entire matrix. Every other philosopher's account of singularity can be understood as a perturbation of his. Along the Eternity axis, the eigenvector runs through Maimonides: his argument that absolute atemporality follows necessarily from necessary existence and divine simplicity is the most logically tight version of a claim all his neighbours share. Whitehead is the eigenvector of the suppressed eigenvalue — the direction the matrix is trying not to go. His dipolar God, with a primordial nature that is eternal and a consequent nature that is genuinely temporal, growing as the world unfolds, is the column most orthogonal to the dominant eigenvectors, which is exactly why it matters. The SEP entry notes that Whitehead's process theology represents a significant neoclassical revision to perfect being theology, dropping immutability and impassibility to save genuine divine personhood. In eigenvector terms, Whitehead is the outlier who prevents the Philosopher Matrix from collapsing into a near-singular object — a single massive eigenvalue of Unity-and-Eternity — and ensures instead that the tradition's ten-dimensional space remains genuinely open.

The transpose of the Philosopher Matrix reveals a different structure. The original matrix reads each philosopher across all ten properties; the transpose reads each property across all ten philosophers. Take the first row in the transpose — Existence, God's ontological status, read across all ten columns. It runs: not a being at all (Laozi), the Form of the Good surpassing being in dignity and power (Plato), no inherently existing ground of any kind (Nagarjuna), the One beyond being and thought (Plotinus), pure Being-Consciousness-Bliss and nothing else (Shankara), the necessary being whose existence and essence are identical (Maimonides), Absolute Being of which everything else is a self-disclosure (Ibn Arabi), the one infinite substance identical with Nature (Spinoza), a postulate of practical reason unknowable theoretically (Kant), a unique dipolar actual entity neither supreme being nor Being itself (Whitehead). This row is a gradient from pure negation through pure affirmation through formal ontology through the paradox of a limited participant. It reveals that the ten properties are not asking ten different questions but one question from ten different angles, which means the matrix's row space is less rich than its column space. The transpose makes this visible: the columns are genuinely independent, the rows are variations on a theme. In the SEP entry's language, all ten rows are asking some version of the same question about metaphysical, axiological, and soteriological ultimacy; the columns are the philosophically irreducible answers to that question.

The SVD of the Philosopher Matrix factors it into three components. The first and largest singular value captures the dominant pattern: the near-universal convergence on a divine that is non-dual, atemporal, beyond predication, and prior to moral categories. This is the shared assumption that holds the table together and that the SEP entry recognises as a recurring feature across the models it surveys — most models of Brahman, God, and the Dao converge on some version of metaphysical ultimacy understood as unity and ground of being. The second singular value captures the axis of divergence: personal versus impersonal, responsive versus necessary, relational versus absolute. Along this axis, Whitehead and Ibn Arabi stand at one pole — a God saturated with love, responsiveness, and self-disclosure — while Nagarjuna and Spinoza stand at the other — a ground indifferent to response, identical with necessity, beyond loving. The SEP entry maps exactly this axis in its taxonomy of models: the divide between classical theists who prioritise the Greek perfections (immutability, impassibility, atemporality) and neoclassical theists and process theologians who prioritise genuine divine personhood and responsiveness. The third singular value captures a subtler pattern: the axis of knowability, running from Laozi's radical silence through Plotinus's mystical henosis through Maimonides's systematic negation to Spinoza's confident geometric demonstration. What the SVD reveals, and what the SEP entry's section on challenges to modeling ultimacy independently confirms, is that the knowability axis is orthogonal to both the unity axis and the personal-versus-impersonal axis: you can be radically apophatic and radically personal (Ibn Arabi), or radically apophatic and radically impersonal (Laozi, Nagarjuna), or radically cataphatic and radically impersonal (Spinoza), or radically cataphatic and moderately personal (Whitehead).

The inverse of the Philosopher Matrix represents, allegorically, the transformation that undoes the philosophical tradition's work — that starts from the answer and recovers the question. The inverse is silence: the condition of mind before any of these ten people sat down to think. But that silence is not empty. It is the silence from which the Tao cannot be distinguished. The matrix and its inverse are both pointing at the same thing, which is the thing neither can capture. This is the deepest structural irony of the entire table: the matrix that maps human thought about ultimacy is itself an image of what ultimacy is on nearly every account within it — a totality that cannot be inverted, a ground that cannot be stepped outside of, a system whose determinant is not quite zero and not quite computable. The SEP entry notes that those who insist on the ineffability of ultimacy may be right that silence is the most accurate response, yet they "literally disappear from the conversation" — they cannot make their point without using the very conceptual machinery they are refusing. The inverse matrix has exactly this structure: it exists in principle but cannot be written down.

The PCA of the Philosopher Matrix projects its ten-dimensional space onto two dimensions of maximum variance. The first principal component is apophasis: how strongly does each philosopher insist that ultimacy cannot be positively described? Highest loadings: Nagarjuna, Laozi, Maimonides. Lowest: Spinoza, Whitehead. The second principal component is personal relation: does this philosopher's ultimacy know, love, respond, or care about individual beings? Highest loadings: Whitehead, Ibn Arabi, Kant (in the moral postulate sense). Lowest: Spinoza, Nagarjuna, Laozi. In the resulting two-dimensional PCA space, the ten philosophers form a recognisable topology. Whitehead occupies one extreme: maximum personal relation, minimum apophasis. Nagarjuna occupies the other: maximum apophasis, minimum personal relation. Spinoza and Laozi cluster near each other in the low-relation, moderate-apophasis quadrant, despite being separated by a millennium and two entirely different conceptual vocabularies — the geometry of the PCA space revealing a structural kinship that intellectual history might otherwise obscure. Most striking is the cluster of Maimonides and Ibn Arabi: both high on apophasis (God cannot be named, every positive predicate violates divine simplicity), yet both insisting on genuine divine intimacy with the creature (God closer to every human being than their jugular vein; the divine Names addressing each creature personally). The SEP entry notes precisely this tension in Abrahamic perfect being theology — how can the God who led Israel out of Egypt also be immutable and impassible? Maimonides and Ibn Arabi are the two thinkers who feel this tension most acutely and develop the most sophisticated responses to it. Their proximity in PCA space is not accidental. It is the geometry of a shared problem.

The Prophet Matrix is the same shape — ten by ten, one hundred cells — but the structure of its values is categorically different. Where the Philosopher Matrix encodes positions arrived at through argument, the Prophet Matrix encodes positions arrived at through encounter. The SEP entry's section on models of what is ultimate carefully distinguishes "philosophical models" — such as Aristotle's Unmoved Mover or Plotinus's One — from "religious models" that purport to capture an understanding of reality as it is believed within a living tradition, citing key texts, figures, and practices. The Prophet Matrix is a grid of religious models in this sense: each cell is a report from within a tradition, not a deduction from first principles. The distinction matters algebraically. The columns of the Philosopher Matrix are independent because each philosopher develops a distinct logical structure; the columns of the Prophet Matrix are independent because each prophet speaks from a different civilisational matrix, a different sacred language, a different grammar of encounter with ultimacy.

The trace of the Prophet Matrix runs: Akhenaten on Nature (the Aten as sole source of all life and light — the divine as what the sun does, not what it is), Zarathustra on Revelation (visionary dialogue with Ahura Mazda, a conversation not a dictation, the Gathas recording questions and doubts alongside illuminations), Moses on Singularity (absolute monotheism as covenant loyalty, not metaphysical argument — no other gods before me), Homer on Creation (the gods created nothing, the world simply is, the divine governs without having made), Confucius on Transcendence (Heaven is silent, communicating through the pattern of history and the moral intuitions of the cultivated heart), Vyasa on Demand (svadharma — righteous action offered to God without attachment to results, the Gita's central practical teaching), Buddha on Justice (karma as self-executing natural law, suffering continues until liberation, no divine judge), Jesus on Love (God suffers with us, the cross as divine solidarity, the most theologically audacious claim about divine suffering in either matrix), Muhammad on Salvation (total surrender to God, the straight path — not union but the permanent intimacy of the servant with the divine master), Nanak on Destiny (the soul merges with God in sachkhand — homecoming, like a flame dwelling in light, the liberated soul singing the glory it was always made to sing). The trace of the Prophet Matrix is a hymn: it begins with light and ends with song, and in between it passes through law, silence, duty, karma, suffering, surrender, and return. It is the prophetic tradition's most compressed account of what ultimacy is like when encountered rather than argued.

The eigenvalues of the Prophet Matrix cluster differently from those of the Philosopher Matrix. The dominant eigenvalue here corresponds not to Singularity but to Revelation — the fact that in nine of ten columns, ultimacy breaks its silence to reach the human. The Aten speaks in light; Ahura Mazda speaks in visionary dialogue; YHWH speaks in thunder, law, and the name that names itself; the Olympian gods speak in dreams and sudden epiphanies; Heaven speaks in the pattern of the seasons and the moral intuitions of the sage; Krishna speaks directly to Arjuna on the eve of battle; the Buddha's awakening, though not revelation in the conventional sense, is the communication of what was always true but unheard; God speaks through Jesus's life, death, and resurrection; the Qur'an is the most linguistically specific divine speech in either matrix, every word divine; the Shabad of Sikhism is divine music poured through a tuned human instrument. The SEP entry describes this distinction between the God of Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob — who acts in history, hears prayers, and intervenes — and the God of the philosophers, who is immutable, impassible, and atemporal. The dominant eigenvalue of the Prophet Matrix is precisely this intervening, revealing, historically present God — and it is far larger than any eigenvalue of the Philosopher Matrix, meaning the prophetic tradition is less internally differentiated along this axis than the philosophical tradition is along any of its axes.

The eigenvectors of the Prophet Matrix are more surprising than those of the Philosopher Matrix. The eigenvector of the Revelation eigenvalue is not Moses or Muhammad — the two figures most associated with verbal divine speech — but Nanak, because Nanak's column holds the revelation-structure in its most self-aware and comprehensive form. Nanak's God speaks as music; the Guru Granth Sahib is meant to be sung rather than argued; and the divine Word (Shabad) is understood to be prior to and encompassing of all other forms of revelation — including the Torah, the Gospels, and the Qur'an. Nanak's column has the highest projection onto the Revelation eigenvector because it is the column most explicitly about the nature of divine communication as such, rather than the content of any particular communication. The eigenvector of the Transcendence-and-Nearness eigenvalue belongs to Muhammad: the Qur'an's pairing of "there is nothing like unto Him" with "we are closer to him than his jugular vein" is the most formally precise statement of the paradox that the SEP entry identifies as central to all Abrahamic perfect being theology — the tension between the God of Greek philosophical perfection and the God of personal historical intervention. Homer is the negative eigenvector: the column with the lowest alignment with both dominant directions, pointing toward the suppressed eigenvalue of divine plurality and fate. In the SEP entry's taxonomy, Homer's gods are neither metaphysically, axiologically, nor soteriologically ultimate in Schellenberg's sense — they did not create the world, they cannot override fate, and they promise no final liberation. Homer's column is the matrix's outlier, and its outlier status is algebraically exact.

The transpose of the Prophet Matrix is again revelatory in a different way from that of the Philosopher Matrix. Read the row for Demand across all ten prophets and you get: nothing at all (Akhenaten — the Aten requires only gratitude and the reception of beauty), good thoughts good words good deeds (Zarathustra), six hundred and thirteen commandments (Moses — the most comprehensive legal revelation in the matrix, governing every domain of life), ritual piety and social excellence (Homer), ritual propriety and human-heartedness (Confucius — li and ren, the outer form and the inner disposition that gives it life), righteous action offered without attachment (Vyasa), the Noble Eightfold Path (Buddha — the most psychologically sophisticated of all the Demand formulations, targeting the mind rather than the law), love God and love your neighbour including your enemy (Jesus — the highest ethical bar in the matrix, achievable only through grace), total submission through the Five Pillars and Sharia (Muhammad — the most comprehensive and universally applicable system), remember work and share (Nanak — the most communitarian and egalitarian, embodied in the langar that feeds everyone regardless of caste or creed). This single row exhibits more variance than any row in the Philosopher Matrix: from Akhenaten's zero demand to Moses's legal code to Jesus's two commandments to Muhammad's comprehensive system to Nanak's three verbs. The transpose reveals the deepest structural fact about the Prophet Matrix: the prophets disagree far more violently about what ultimacy requires of human beings than they disagree about what ultimacy is.

The SVD of the Prophet Matrix reveals a dominant singular value much larger than the second, meaning the prophetic tradition is closer to rank-one coherence than the philosophical tradition — its variance is concentrated in a single direction more completely. The first singular vector explains an enormous proportion of the total variance: nearly every cell, across nearly every row and column, participates in the same basic pattern of an absolute ground that speaks, demands, judges, loves, and promises the soul a home. The SEP entry's section on responses to the diversity of models identifies exactly this structure when it notes that Ramakrishna decided there is no need to choose between the models because each is a finite path to an infinite reality, and that all the traditions are grasping different aspects of the same infinite complexity. The SVD translates this intuition into a structural claim: the first singular vector is the shared grammar of revelation, and it accounts for most of the matrix's content. The low-variance residual — what the first singular vector cannot explain — is concentrated in three columns: Homer (whose gods didn't create anything and cannot overcome fate), the Buddha (whose ultimacy is explicitly not a God and whose Demand is psychological rather than theistic), and Confucius (whose Heaven is so reticent it barely speaks). These three columns are the matrix's outliers, keeping its effective rank higher than its dominant structure would suggest.

Now compare the two matrices directly. Their traces encode the deepest structural difference between the two traditions. The philosopher's trace is made of conceptual positions: impersonal ground, effortless causation, absolute unity, necessary being, cosmic love through Names, geometric truth, moral postulate, dipolar participation. The prophet's trace is made of events: blinding light, wrestling with an angel, receiving dictation from a voice that identified itself as existence itself, watching gods weep at deaths they could not prevent, the silence of Heaven through the turning seasons, Krishna speaking directly to a warrior on the eve of battle, awakening under a tree, the cross, a night in a cave on a mountain near Mecca, emerging from a river with a new name. The philosopher's trace is a map of positions; the prophet's trace is a map of moments. This is the first and most fundamental difference between the two matrices, and the SEP entry's distinction between philosophical and religious models of ultimacy is exactly the distinction between the two traces.

The eigenvalues of the two matrices tell the most striking story. Both have a large dominant eigenvalue corresponding to divine unity. But the second eigenvalues diverge sharply. In the Philosopher Matrix, the second eigenvalue corresponds to Eternity: the second most powerful invariant in the philosophical tradition is the claim that ultimacy is outside time — atemporal, unchanging, the Still Point from which temporal succession flows. In the Prophet Matrix, the second eigenvalue corresponds to Revelation: the second most powerful invariant in the prophetic tradition is the claim that ultimacy breaks its silence to reach the human, speaking into time, acting in history, becoming vulnerable to it. These two second eigenvalues are in genuine tension. A God who is strictly outside time cannot speak into time the way YHWH speaks from the burning bush, cannot send Gabriel to dictate a text in seventh-century Arabia, cannot die on a cross in first-century Judea. And yet this is precisely what the prophetic matrix insists. The SEP entry identifies exactly this tension as the founding puzzle of perfect being theology: how can the God of Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob — who acts in history, hears prayers, and leads people out of Egypt — also be the immutable, impassible, atemporal God of Greek philosophical perfection? The tension between the Philosopher Matrix's second eigenvalue (Eternity) and the Prophet Matrix's second eigenvalue (Revelation) is not a logical error in either matrix. It is the central productive tension of the entire theological tradition, the problem that every major theologian of the Abrahamic traditions — Augustine, Aquinas, Avicenna, Maimonides, Ibn Arabi, Ghazali — has been working on ever since the two traditions met in the Hellenistic era.

The eigenvectors compared reveal the deepest structural parallel. The Philosopher Matrix's dominant eigenvector runs through Plotinus: the thinker who most completely embodies what the philosophical tradition is trying to say about ultimacy, the One beyond being, beyond thought, prior to all names, the inexhaustible source from which all unity derives. The Prophet Matrix's dominant eigenvector runs through Nanak: the figure who most self-consciously holds together the entire prophetic tradition by dissolving its divisions, insisting that the One Being is prior to Hindu and Muslim, male and female, idol and mosque. Plotinus speaks of a One that cannot know itself without splitting in two, that causes by the sheer superabundance of what it is. Nanak speaks of an Ik Onkar — One Being — that creates through the divine Word and is experienced in every breath. They are not the same. Plotinus's One is so radically impersonal that even knowing and loving are denied it; Nanak's Waheguru is the Wonderful Lord who turns toward the soul in grace before the soul has turned toward the divine. But they are the most structurally similar eigenvectors across the two matrices: both point toward a divine ground that is prior to all names, accessible through a kind of inward emptying or remembrance, and identical with the soul's deepest nature. The SEP entry notes that one response to the diversity of models is to read them all as grasping different aspects of a single infinite reality too full to be captured by any one tradition. In eigenvector terms, Plotinus and Nanak are the two thinkers — one philosophical, one prophetic — whose columns most completely embody this shared underlying direction.

The SVD comparison shows that the Philosopher Matrix has a more distributed singular value spectrum — its variance is spread across more dimensions — while the Prophet Matrix is dominated more strongly by a single large singular value. Philosophers disagree with each other more fundamentally than prophets do. The reason is structural and the SEP entry implicitly confirms it. Philosophers have no external authority; each builds from first principles, and so they can arrive at positions as different as Spinoza's Deus sive Natura and Whitehead's dipolar actual entity, or as opposite as Nagarjuna's dissolution of all ultimacy-concepts and Maimonides's necessary being whose essence and existence are identical. Prophets, however, all share the experience of encounter — or at minimum the structure of encounter, the claim that something reached toward them before they reached toward it — and that shared structure constrains the output. You can disagree violently about what ultimacy demands (Akhenaten: nothing; Moses: six hundred and thirteen commandments; Nanak: three verbs) while agreeing that ultimacy is one, that it is present to human experience, and that the soul has a home to return to. The Prophet Matrix's higher coherence reflects not agreement on specifics but agreement on the basic grammar of revelation — the grammar that the SEP entry associates with religious models as distinct from purely philosophical ones.

The PCA comparison is perhaps the most visually striking. In the philosopher PCA space, the axes are apophasis (can ultimacy be described?) and personal relation (does ultimacy respond to individuals?). In the prophet PCA space, the axes are divine agency (does ultimacy act in history?) and eschatological specificity (how detailed is the account of what awaits the soul?). These four axes are related but not identical, and their relationships reveal the deep connections between the two traditions. Apophasis and divine agency are inversely correlated across both matrices: the more a tradition insists ultimacy cannot be described, the less it typically insists that ultimacy intervenes in specific historical events. Nagarjuna and Laozi are high on apophasis and low on divine agency; Moses and Muhammad are low on apophasis and high on divine agency. Personal relation and eschatological specificity are positively correlated: traditions that emphasise the soul's intimate relationship with ultimacy also tend to elaborate on the soul's destiny after death. Whitehead and Jesus are both high on personal relation; they are also both high on the claim that what is genuinely lived is permanently preserved. The SEP entry, following Schellenberg, proposes three dimensions of ultimacy: metaphysical, axiological, and soteriological. The four PCA axes map onto these three dimensions with notable precision. Apophasis and divine agency both bear on metaphysical ultimacy — on whether ultimacy is the most fundamental fact about the nature of things. Personal relation bears on axiological ultimacy — on whether ultimacy has unsurpassably great value in a personal and moral sense. Eschatological specificity bears on soteriological ultimacy — on whether ultimacy is the source of an ultimate good, a final liberation or salvation. The four axes of the combined PCA space are thus the Schellenbergian three-part account of ultimacy decomposed into its principal components — which is, perhaps, what one should expect when the content of the matrices was shaped throughout by thinkers grappling with precisely these three dimensions of what it means to be ultimate.

The inverse comparison is philosophically the richest. The inverse of the Philosopher Matrix is silence — the state before argument, before thought, before the first question about what kind of thing God might be. The inverse of the Prophet Matrix is forgetting — the state before encounter, the world in which no bush burned without being consumed, no sun was proclaimed sole God, no river parted, no voice said "Be!" and created something from nothing. The product of each matrix with its own inverse is the identity — and in this allegorical algebra, the identity matrix is ultimacy itself: the transformation that leaves everything unchanged, the ground that gives each thing its own nature without adding anything to it, the presence whose absence is the only impossibility. The SEP entry observes that those who embrace the diversity of models — comparative theologians, the Theology Without Walls movement, Ramakrishna's pluralism — read the entire body of models as data, and their number and inconsistencies not as a deal-breaker but as an interpretive challenge: an infinite reality needs all the models to help us see it. In matrix terms, this is the claim that the union of the column spaces of both matrices — the combined twenty-column array of all twenty models of ultimacy surveyed here — spans more of the conceptual space of the divine than either matrix spans separately. The combined array is higher-rank than either alone. The philosophical tradition and the prophetic tradition are not redundant with each other. They are linearly independent. Together, they span more of what it might mean to be ultimate than either can span alone.
